Dr. Neil DeGrasse Tyson

A man with a passion for truth

"The good thing about science is that it's true whether or not you believe in it."
Neil DeGrasse Tyson

 

Here is a time line of Dr. DeGrasse Tyson's life.

 
 

  • 1958 - Born in Manhattan, New York.
  • 1967 - His first trip to Hayden Planetarium.
  • 1976 - Graduated from Bronx High School of Science.
  • 1980 - Graduated from Harvard University with a Bachelor's Degree in Physics.
  • 1983 - Received a Master of Arts in astronomy from University of Texas as Austin.
  • 1986 - Appointed as a lecturer in astronomy at the University of Maryland.
  • 1991 - Awarded a Doctorate of Philosophy in astrophysics.
  • 1995 - Began writing the column 'Universe' for the 'Natural History' magazine which went on to become very popular. The magazine released a special edition titled ‘City of Stars’ (2002), in which Tyson popularized the term "Manhattanhenge" which describes the two days in a year on which the evening sun aligns with the street grid in Manhattan.
  • 2001 - President George W Bush Tyson as a member of the 12-member commission to study the Future of the US Aerospace Industry.
  • 2004 - President Bush once again appointed him in 2004 to serve on a 9-member commission to the Implementation of the United States Space Exploration Policy. He also hosted the four-part miniseries ‘Origins’ of the PBS ‘Nova’ series.
  • 2006 - He was a speaker at the ‘Beyond Belief: Science, Religion, Reason and Survival’ symposium in November 2006. He became a regular on the series ‘The Universe’, broadcast on ‘The History Channel’.
  • 2009 - In 2009, he again collaborated with Donald Goldsmith as the narrator on the documentary ‘400 Years of the Telescope’ which premiered on PBS.
  • At Present - Serves as the Frederick P. Rose Director of the Hayden Planetarium at the Rose Center for Earth and Space. He is also a research associate in the department of astrophysics at the American Museum of Natural History.

Awards & Achievements

  • 2004 - Received the NASA Distinguished Public Service Medal, the highest civilian honor bestowed by NASA.
  • 2009 - Presented with the Douglas S. Morrow Public Outreach Award from the Space Foundation for significant contributions to public awareness of space programs. The same year he also received Isaac Asimov Award from the American Humanist Association.
